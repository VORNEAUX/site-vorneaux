How much should you actually charge as a freelancer?

The formula almost every rate calculator gets wrong — because it starts from your invoice instead of your tax return.

Somewhere between your last invoice and your last tax bill, there's a number you never actually calculated: what you kept. Not what you billed — what landed in your account after tax, after the slow month in August, after the client who paid net-60 instead of net-15. Most freelancers set their rate once, based on a number that felt fair, and then spend the next year quietly discovering it wasn't.

That's not a discipline problem. It's a math problem — and it starts with how the rate was calculated in the first place.

The mistake baked into every rate calculator

Search "freelance rate calculator" and you'll find dozens of tools that do the same thing: take a target annual income, divide it by roughly 2,000 working hours, and hand you an hourly number. It looks precise. It isn't.

That calculation assumes three things that are almost never true for an independent worker:

  • That you'll bill 40 hours a week, every week. No freelancer does. Between admin, proposals, invoicing, and the inevitable slow stretch, actual billable time is usually 50–65% of total working hours.
  • That the number you land on is what you keep. It isn't — it's what you're paid before tax, before software costs, before the portion of every invoice that already belongs to the government.
  • That every year looks the same. It doesn't. Client churn, seasonal demand, and the time it takes to replace a lost contract all eat into the "2,000 hours a year" the calculator assumed.

The result is a rate that looks reasonable on a proposal and quietly underpays you all year — because it was built forward from a guess, instead of backward from what you actually need to keep.

A rate isn't a number you feel comfortable quoting. It's a number you've already tested against your tax bill.

The tax-inclusive formula

The fix is to reverse the calculation. Instead of starting with an hourly number and hoping it covers everything, start with what you need to keep — and work backward through taxes and billable hours until you arrive at the number that actually protects it.

The formula

Hourly rate = (Target take-home income ÷ (1 − effective tax rate)) ÷ realistic billable hours per year

Three inputs, in order: 1) what you want to keep after tax, 2) your actual effective tax rate — not your bracket, your real average rate — and 3) the hours you can genuinely bill, not the hours in a calendar.

The second input is where most freelancers guess. Tax rates aren't flat — they depend on income level, entity structure, and country, and they change what "target income" actually means in take-home terms. A freelancer in one country keeping 78% of every dollar after tax has a completely different rate math than one keeping 62%. Guessing here is where the formula quietly breaks.

That gap — between hours worked and hours billed — is the single most underestimated variable in freelance pricing. It's also the one most calculators skip entirely, because "hours per year" is easier to model as a fixed number than as the moving target it actually is.

What actually eats into billable time

  • Proposals and discovery calls that don't convert
  • Invoicing, bookkeeping, and client admin
  • The gap between finishing one project and starting the next
  • Time off you're not paid for, because nobody else is paying it

A worked example, start to finish

Take a freelancer who wants to keep $70,000 after tax, in a jurisdiction where their effective tax rate — federal, state, and self-employment tax combined — comes to roughly 28%. And say a realistic year gives them 1,100 billable hours once admin and downtime are subtracted.

$70,000 ÷ (1 − 0.28) = $97,222 in gross freelance income needed. $97,222 ÷ 1,100 billable hours = $88.38/hour, not the $35/hour a naive "$70,000 ÷ 2,000 hours" calculation would have suggested.

That's not a rounding error. It's the difference between a rate that funds a comfortable year and one that quietly runs a deficit disguised as a full calendar.

Questions worth answering before you set a rate

What's a fair hourly rate for a freelancer?
There's no universal number. It depends on your target take-home pay, your effective tax rate, and how many hours you can genuinely bill once downtime and admin are subtracted — a rate that looks generous on paper can still leave you underpaid once those are factored in.
Should freelance rates include tax?
Yes — your rate should be calculated backward from the income you want to keep after tax, not forward from a number that simply feels competitive. Otherwise part of every invoice is quietly funding a bill you haven't set aside for.
How many hours a year can a freelancer actually bill?
Most independent workers can realistically bill 50–65% of their total working hours once time off, admin, proposals, and slow stretches are accounted for — well short of the 40-hour week many rate calculators assume by default.
