Why your ChatGPT prompts keep failing

It's not the model. It's that most prompts describe a topic instead of a task — and the model can only answer the question it was actually asked.

Everyone eventually says some version of the same thing: "ChatGPT is fine, but it gives me generic answers." It's rarely said as a complaint about the model. It's said as a shrug — as if generic is just what AI does once the novelty wears off.

It isn't. Generic is what AI does when the prompt is generic. The model isn't holding back a better answer; it's giving you the most statistically average response to an average-shaped question. Change the shape of the question, and the ceiling on the answer changes with it.

The real reason your answers feel generic

A prompt like "write me a marketing email" gives the model almost nothing to work with: no role, no audience, no reasoning method, no constraints, no format. So it defaults to the safest possible interpretation — competent, forgettable, and interchangeable with what a thousand other people got from the exact same three words.

The gap isn't intelligence. It's specification. A model can reason at an expert level, but only about the problem you actually describe — and "write me a marketing email" isn't a problem, it's a category.

You're not talking to an oracle. You're briefing a very capable freelancer who's never met your business and can't ask a single follow-up question.

The five parts of a prompt that actually works

Every prompt that consistently produces expert-level output — regardless of the task — tends to specify the same five things. Miss any one, and the model has to guess, and its guess is always the average case.

  1. Role — who the model should reason as (a tax attorney, a senior copywriter, a skeptical editor), which sets the vocabulary and standards it reaches for.
  2. Context — the specific situation, audience, or constraint that makes this task different from the generic version of itself.
  3. Reasoning method — how it should think through the problem: step by step, by weighing tradeoffs, by working backward from a goal.
  4. Constraints — what's off-limits: length, tone, things to avoid, non-negotiables.
  5. Output format — the exact shape the answer should take, so you're not reformatting it yourself afterward.

Before and after, side by side

Here's the same request, once written the way most people write it, and once written with all five parts specified.

Generic prompt

"Write a marketing email for my new productivity app."

Framework prompt

"As a direct-response copywriter who's launched 50+ SaaS products, write a launch email for a productivity app aimed at overwhelmed freelancers. Reason through the reader's biggest objection first, then address it before the CTA. Keep it under 150 words, no exclamation points, one clear CTA. Format as subject line + body."

The second version isn't longer because longer is better — it's longer because it removes every place the model would otherwise have to guess. That's the actual skill in "prompt engineering": not cleverness, just refusing to leave the important decisions to chance.

Why a prompt system beats a prompt habit

Once you know the five parts, the temptation is to rewrite them from scratch every time — and that works, until you're doing it for the twentieth task of the week and quietly reverting to shortcuts. The people who get consistently strong output from AI aren't the ones who are best at improvising a good prompt. They're the ones who stopped improvising.

That's the difference between a habit and a system: a habit is something you try to remember to do well; a system does it for you, the same way every time, regardless of how rushed you are. Questions worth answering before your next prompt

Why does ChatGPT give generic answers?
Because a generic prompt only describes the topic, not the reasoning path the model should take, the role it should adopt, or the format the answer needs — so the model fills those gaps with the safest, most average response it can generate.
What is a prompt framework?
A repeatable structure — role, context, reasoning method, constraints, and output format — that you fill in for any task, instead of writing a new freeform request from scratch every time.
Do I need to learn to code to write good AI prompts?
No. Prompt engineering is a writing and reasoning skill, not a technical one — it comes down to being specific about role, context, method, and format, which anyone can learn regardless of background.
